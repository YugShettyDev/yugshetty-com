The Attention Economy Is Bankrupting Our Minds

We've built an entire economy around capturing human attention, and we're only beginning to understand the cost.

Okay so I've been thinking about this a lot lately. I don't actually use Instagram or TikTok—I made a deliberate choice early on because I'd rather spend my time learning skills, building projects, and figuring out my future than getting caught in endless feeds. But here's the thing: I see what it does to people around me all the time.

I'll be in a room with friends and someone will pull out their phone to show me something, and then suddenly they're scrolling for ten minutes while the rest of us wait. Or I'll notice peers talking about how they lost an entire afternoon to Instagram or YouTube Shorts without meaning to.

Even for me though, YouTube Shorts sometimes catch me off guard. I go to watch one specific video and suddenly I'm three or four shorts deep and I've lost 20 minutes. And that's YouTube—I'm not even on the platforms that are really engineered for addiction.

This is what we call the attention economy. And honestly, I think most people don't really understand how deliberate and how deep this goes.

What is the Attention Economy Actually?

So basically, the attention economy is this: if a company can capture your attention for long enough, they can sell that attention to advertisers. Your eyeballs are the product. This is not a new idea anymore—everyone says this. But I think people don't really feel how weird this is.

Think about how different this is from like... any other economy. When you buy a coffee, you exchange money for coffee. That's straightforward. But when you use Instagram, you exchange your time and your attention and your data for a service that seems free but is actually being monetized by the platform in the background.

The platforms—Google, Meta, TikTok, all of them—they've built entire teams of engineers and psychologists whose whole job is to figure out how to keep you on the app longer. Not to make the app better necessarily. Just to make it harder to leave.

This is not accidental. This is engineered.

How Does This Actually Work in Your Brain?

Okay so there's this thing called variable reward schedules. I learned about this from BJ Fogg who studies behavior change. It comes from psychology research going back decades.

Basically, if you want someone to keep doing something, you shouldn't reward them every single time. Instead, you should reward them unpredictably. Sometimes they get a reward, sometimes they don't. But they never know which time will be the reward.

This is literally how slot machines work. You pull the lever, sometimes you win money, sometimes you don't. But that unpredictability is what makes it addictive. Your brain gets hooked on the possibility of the reward, not the reward itself.

I've watched this happen to people around me on Instagram. They scroll and scroll and sometimes they see a post from someone they actually care about, sometimes they see an ad, sometimes they see a random person's content. They never know what's coming next. So they keep scrolling.

TikTok is apparently even worse because the algorithm is supposedly so good that sometimes people scroll into content that is exactly what they want to see. And then the next one is slightly off. And then the next one is perfect again. Their brain is like... wait maybe if I scroll more there will be another perfect one.

I see this play out in real time with people I know, and honestly it's kind of concerning to watch because they're aware it's happening but they can't really stop.

There's another thing called the recency bias. Basically your brain gives more weight to things that happened recently. This is why if you have a bad day, the whole week feels bad. Not because bad things happened all week, but because the bad day is recent and your brain is still thinking about it.

Social media platforms use this too. They push notifications to your phone. Someone liked your post. Someone commented. These things ping your brain and make you feel like you need to check. And when you do check, you're back in the app, and now you're vulnerable to scrolling for another 30 minutes.

The Real Cost

Here's where it gets actually concerning though.

There was research done by Gloria Mark at UC Irvine. She studied office workers and how long they could focus on one task without getting distracted. In 2004, the average was about 11 minutes. By 2012, it was down to like 3 minutes. We're getting worse at focusing.

And it's not just that we're distracted. It's that our brains are getting rewired. There's research showing that people who spend a lot of time on social media have more anxiety and more depression. Not because social media is inherently bad, but because of how it messes with your dopamine system.

Your brain releases dopamine when something good happens. It's the reward chemical. When you get a like on Instagram, dopamine goes up a little bit. But here's the problem: your brain adapts to dopamine. It's called tolerance. So you need more and more dopamine to feel satisfied.

This is why a like that would have made you happy two years ago doesn't feel the same now. You've built tolerance. You need more. You need more engagement. You need a comment instead of just a like. You need a comment from someone you actually know. Your brain keeps chasing bigger hits.

There's also this thing called FOMO—fear of missing out. Psychologists have studied this extensively. When you see other people doing things or having experiences, there's a real psychological cost to feeling left out. Social media basically mainlines FOMO directly into your brain all day.

Like you see your friend's vacation photos. You see your colleague got promoted. You see someone's relationship. And even if your life is actually fine, there's this low-level anxiety that you're not doing enough, not experiencing enough, not living enough.

And the mental cost of all this is real. Psychologists are actually worried now. Depression rates are going up. Anxiety is going up. Attention spans are going down. Sleep is getting worse because people are on their phones right before bed.

Why Is This So Hard to Resist?

The thing that gets me is that even when you know what's happening, it's still so hard to resist.

I read about all this stuff. I understand the variable reward schedules. I know that TikTok's algorithm is designed to keep me hooked. And I still pick up my phone and lose two hours.

This is because willpower is not actually a strong solution to this problem. Psychologists used to think willpower was unlimited—like a muscle you could always exercise. But actually it's more like... you have a limited amount of willpower every day, and if you're spending it fighting against apps that have million-dollar engineering teams designing ways to beat your willpower, you're probably going to lose.

The better approach is to change your environment. Delete the apps. Turn off notifications. Put your phone in another room. Because if the trigger isn't there, you don't have to use willpower.

But even that is hard because these companies are really good at making you want to use their apps. And also, some of these apps are genuinely useful. Like, I use Instagram to stay in touch with people and I use YouTube to learn things. So completely deleting them doesn't feel realistic for a lot of people.

What Actually Happens If You Just Keep Going?

I think the scariest thing is that we don't fully know yet.

Like, we have a whole generation of kids who grew up with smartphones. The oldest ones are only in their late twenties now. We don't have the long-term data yet on what this does to your brain, your mental health, your ability to form relationships, any of it.

There are some early warning signs though. The suicide rate among teenagers went up significantly starting around 2010. That's when smartphones really became mainstream. Social media use went up at the same time. There's research showing correlation, though it's hard to prove causation.

And then there's the attention thing. If our attention spans keep shrinking, what does that mean for learning? For relationships? For being able to focus on hard problems?

Technology has given us so much. The internet is incredible. But we've let companies optimize the internet for engagement rather than wellbeing, and I think we're starting to see the bill come due.

What Can You Actually Do?

I don't have a perfect answer here. I'm not some expert who never uses social media.

But I think the first step is just being aware that this is engineered. You're not weak for finding these apps hard to resist. You're not lazy. You're a human being with a normal human brain being attacked by teams of very smart engineers.

Some things I've tried or thought about:

  • For YouTube Shorts specifically, I try to watch with a purpose and close the app after. Easier said than done.
  • Watching how my peers get pulled in has made me way more intentional about any app time at all.
  • The whole choice to skip Instagram and TikTok in the first place is basically the biggest thing I've done. It meant missing some networking stuff probably, but it also meant not having that constant feed in my face every day.
  • When I do watch videos on YouTube, I try to be conscious that the next-video autoplay is engineered to keep me there.

Does this solve the problem? No. But it helps. And honestly the fact that I never got on Instagram in the first place seems like the best decision I made, because at least I'm not fighting the habit.

Also, I think we need to talk more about this at a bigger level. Like, we regulate tobacco and alcohol because they're addictive and bad for you. Maybe at some point we regulate social media the same way? There's some countries starting to do this.

But that's a long-term thing. In the short term, you just have to try to protect your own attention.

The Real Thing

I think the scariest part of all this is that we can feel it happening and we're mostly just accepting it.

Like I'll be talking to a friend and they'll be halfway looking at their phone the whole conversation. Not out of rudeness really, just because the pull is there. And they know it's there. I know it's there. But we just sort of... accept it?

We're all slightly less present. We're all slightly less able to focus. We're all slightly more anxious. And we're treating this like it's just normal now.

It is not normal. This is new. And the fact that it feels normal is actually the most concerning thing about it.

We've built an economy around capturing attention because attention is the most valuable resource. And we've been so successful at building it that we've basically forgotten that our attention is supposed to be ours in the first place.

I don't have a neat conclusion here. This is still happening. I'm still fighting it. You're probably still fighting it too.

But maybe the first step is just acknowledging what's actually going on. Not as a secret or a conspiracy. Just as a fact: there are companies that make money when you spend time on their platforms, and they've gotten very, very good at making you want to spend that time.

Once you see it clearly, you can at least try to do something about it.
